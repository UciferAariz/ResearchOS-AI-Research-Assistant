Hook & What It Is

Narration
"Researchers drown in papers. Keyword search misses conceptually related work. Reading every PDF end-to-end is impossible. This is ResearchOS — an AI research assistant that transforms how you find and understand scientific literature. You search papers by meaning, not keywords. You chat with them and get answers grounded in the actual PDF text, with inline citations. You compare methods across papers side by side. And here's what matters: every semantic operation — every embedding, every similarity search, every vector retrieval — runs on an AMD Radeon GPU through ROCm. That's where the speed comes from."
On-screen action
Show the live site's Home page. Slowly pan the cursor down the left sidebar so the navigation items (Discover, Assistant, Reader, Compare, History) are visible. Hold for 2 seconds.
Why this opening: Judges need to understand the problem (researchers drown in papers) and the solution (semantic search + RAG) in the first 25 seconds. Lead with ResearchOS, not the GPU — the GPU payoff comes next.

The AMD GPU Proof (Critical for Scoring)

Narration
"Let me show the hardware. Here's the backend running on an AMD gfx1100 — that's RDNA3, a Radeon RX 7900 XTX class — under ROCm 7.2.1. The embedding model loads on device=cuda, which on AMD hardware maps to the HIP backend. This is real GPU compute, not a soft claim. Here's the throughput: embedding a batch of 10 sentences runs at 555 texts per second on the GPU. A batch of 500 runs at over 3,000 per second — 458 times faster than CPU. That's not a fixed offset; throughput scales with parallelism. That's why the interactive latency stays snappy even under load."

1:05–1:45

Semantic Search (Discover)

Narration
"Now the product in action. In Discover, I search arXiv and PubMed by meaning. I type a query like 'attention mechanisms for long-context transformers.' The query is embedded on the AMD GPU in real time, indexed into ChromaDB, and retrieval is by vector similarity — not keyword matching. Results surface papers with related concepts that a keyword search would miss. And here's a bonus: each result has a 'Similar papers' button. That's pure nearest-neighbor search in embedding space — find the papers closest to this one semantically. Click it."
On-screen action
Click Discover in the sidebar. Type the query attention mechanisms for long-context transformers. Hit search. Let results render (they'll stream in from the backend). Once results appear, hover over a paper card to show the "Similar papers" action. Click it to show the similar papers panel. Hold for 5 seconds.
Demo tip: If search is slow, do a simpler query like transformers or language models. The goal is to show results appearing in real time from a live API call, not to wait 15 seconds. Pre-warming the model in setup ensures snappy responses.
1:45–2:35

Citation-Grounded Chat (Assistant)

Narration
"The Assistant is where it gets powerful. This is retrieval-augmented generation. I ask a question, and the system retrieves from the actual, full PDF text in the corpus — not just abstracts. It chunks the relevant papers, embeds them on the AMD GPU, ranks them by relevance, and streams the answer back with inline citations you can click to jump to the source. Every embedding and retrieval happens on GPU, so latency is sub-second even with thousands of papers. The citations aren't hallucinations; they're grounded in real text."
On-screen action
Click Assistant in the sidebar. Type a question like: What problem does the transformer architecture solve, and how?. Hit send. Watch as:
  • Citations appear in a box on the right (these are retrieved documents).
  • The answer streams in on the left, grounded in those sources.
Once the answer finishes, click one citation to show it jumps back to the source paper in the Reader.
Demo tip: Keep the question specific but open-ended so the answer is substantive. "Tell me about X" usually gets 3–4 sentences grounded in 2–3 papers, which reads well on camera.

Compare & Reader

Narration
"Next is Compare. Paste in two to five arXiv IDs, and the system contrasts their methods, contributions, and results side by side. It's built on LLM text generation, so the comparison reads naturally — not a table, but a narrative. Any paper also opens in the Reader for a grounded summary. And if you have your own PDF — research notes, a preprint, anything — you can upload it. It gets extracted, chunked, embedded on the GPU, and becomes instantly chat-queryable just like any arXiv paper. No proprietary format, no friction."
On-screen action
Click Compare. In the form, paste these two arXiv IDs:
  • 1706.03762 (Attention is All You Need)
  • 1810.04805 (BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers)
Hit "Compare". Let the comparison render. Scroll through it to show 2–3 bullet points. Then briefly switch to a paper in the Reader (click on a search result or a citation) to show the summary view and the Upload PDF panel at the bottom.

Architecture & Infra

Narration
"Here's how it fits together. The Next.js frontend lives on Vercel. It talks over HTTPS to a FastAPI backend running on this AMD notebook, exposed via a Cloudflare Tunnel so it's reachable from anywhere. All the sentence-transformer embeddings run on the AMD GPU; ChromaDB stores the vectors on disk so they persist across restarts. The LLM text generation — chat and compare — is handled by Fireworks AI, a remote API. But here's the key: the latency-sensitive path, the interactive path, the path that matters for responsiveness — that's the embeddings. And that runs on AMD. That's where the GPU buys you speed."
On-screen action
Open README.md in the editor or on GitHub and scroll to the Architecture section (around line 39). Show the ASCII diagram of Next.js → FastAPI → GPU → ChromaDB pipeline.
